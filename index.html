Week 1 Prompt

What is teaching? What is learning? What brought you to teaching and why do you want to be a teacher? What is your vision of teaching and learning?

Teaching is the practice of facilitating meaningful learning experiences that enable students to think critically, be curious, develop skills, and grow holistically. It is a multifaceted job--requiring empathy, creativity, and attentiveness, as well as a deep understanding of curriculum content, pedagogy, and child development. Teaching is not solely defined by imparting knowledge. I believe teaching means knowing students, empowering students, and guiding students as they grow. Biologically, learning is the process of forming new neural pathways and connections in the brain. Behaviourally, it involves acquiring new skills or deepening existing ones through experiences such as instruction, discussion, inquiry, experimentation, and problem-solving. I also believe that learning extends beyond the retention of information; it is most meaningful when students generate new ideas, questions, and perspectives that reshape how they understand the world around them.

My own educational journey shaped my desire to become a teacher. Early on, school was a place of joy, exploration, and creativity. By second grade, after already completing four years of formal education, I was a curious and eager learner. I dreamed of becoming an author, and my teacher supported that passion by laminating my mystery stories so I could share them during “show and tell.” Learning felt investigative and empowering. However, as I progressed into high school, my experience at school became procedural and performance-driven. Grades became my primary source of motivation and identity. The curiosity I once had faded into a fixation on numerical outcomes. My undergraduate studies in Operations Research and Information Engineering marked a turning point. Through courses in coding, mathematical modelling, and statistical simulation, I gained a deeper understanding of how mathematics operates in business, finance, and public services. For the first time in many years, I felt reconnected to utility of what I was learning. I understood the relevance of applied mathematics and how it informs real decisions. This rediscovery ignited my passion for teaching and education. As I now prepare to enter the teaching profession, I am excited to challenge myself to make math and science meaningful to every kind of learner and intersect curriculum content with students' lived experiences.

My vision of teaching is one that is equitable, safe, inspiring, and responsive to individual student needs. My vision of my classroom is an environment where students feel safe, valued, seen and notice their own potential. My experiences as a student-athlete, coach, and Residential Assistant at Trinity College School has shaped this vision. While living in the boarding school residence amongst 50 high school girls from around the world, I came to recognize that students’ identities extend far beyond their participation in the classroom—they are athletes, musicians, writers, leaders, creators, and innovators, who have a rich diversity of backgrounds and lived experiences. To me, teaching is not only about imparting subject knowledge but also about recognizing each student’s strengths, passions, and areas of growth in the classroom and their surrounding communities.

Week 2 Prompts

1. What comes to mind when you hear "classroom management?" Where did you get this impression? What role does it have in teaching and learning?

To me, classroom management employs strategies to capture attention, engage learners, encourage participation, and maintain a safe and respectful learning environment. I developed this impression not only from my own schooling in Ontario but also through my time as an assistant teacher at a private high school. Many of my teachers used clapping techniques, flickering the lights, or even a stern stare to recenter the class’s focus. Yet I have come to believe that classroom management extends far beyond attention-grabbing and behaviour mediation.

As a teaching assistant, I observed mentor teachers who established meaningful connections with students by coaching sports, asking about hobbies, or recognizing individual strengths and challenges. These teachers also opened space for dialogue by discussing expectations at the start of the year and providing students with choices in assignment due dates and project topics. The resulting classroom culture felt collaborative and respectful, which I suspect reduced the frequency of disruptions and behavioural interventions. At the same time, I recognize my perspective is shaped by the uniqueness of that school. Smaller-than-average class sizes and a student body largely composed of families who paid tuition (and therefore often chose to be there) likely influenced classroom dynamics. I suspect the classroom management strategies I observed may have been more effective in that context than they might be in a different one. Recognizing this, I see classroom management as an ongoing process of observation, adaptation, and reflection, rather than a fixed set of techniques.

As discussed in Foundations 1, good classroom management is a prerequisite for productive teaching and learning. When students feel safe, respected, and intrigued, they are more likely to actively participate in class, ask questions, and collaborate with peers. Without reliable classroom management strategies, meaningful instruction is difficult to achieve, as distractions, emotions, and anxiety can easily create barriers between students and the course material.


2. How does a learning community contribute to the learning process?

A learning community contributes to the learning process through mutual respect for mistakes, considerate grading practices, collaborative relationships, cultural awareness, positive colleague relationships, and meaningful connections to the broader community. How People Learn: Brain, Mind, Experience, and School identifies these components as features of community-centred environments. Chapter 6 notes that “learning seems to be enhanced by social norms that value the search for understanding and allow students (and teachers) the freedom to make mistakes” (Brown & Campione, 1994; Cobb et al., 1992). Mistakes are an inevitable and invaluable part of the learning process, and when they are welcomed and constructively reflected upon, they enable growth. The National Research council also emphasizes the importance of fair and considerate grading practices. Systems that publicly distinguish high and low-achieving students risk creating divisive relationships between students while fair, confidential, and growth-oriented grading practices encourage rapport (National Research Council, 146) . Similarly, applying course concepts to students’ lived experiences enhances engagement. How People Learn reminded me that students spend only a small percentage of their time in classrooms compared to home and community environments (National Research Council, 148). This statistic highlights the importance of connection between school and students’ personal lives, extra-curricular activities, community centres, and local businesses. When teachers deliberately create these bridges, learning becomes more relevant and exciting. At the same time, a learning community is also enriched by cultural integration. Chapter 6 also identifies the value of teacher-teacher respect and collaboration, citing multiple academic studies reporting this finding ( National Research Council, 147). Ultimately, a strong learning community is the foundation for deep engagement, positive relationships, and motivation.


3. Why must learning environments be student-centred?

Learning environments must be student-centred in order for students to feel connected, valued, and supported in school. According to How People Learn: Brain, Mind, Experience, and School, student-centred environments thoughtfully consider learners' prior knowledge, skills, attitudes, and beliefs, and build on their existing conceptual and cultural knowledge (National Research Council, p. 133). As a teacher candidate, I am committed to incorporating student-centred strategies in my future classroom. In particular, I plan to use observation and diagnostic teaching to identify misconceptions and tailor instruction to each learner’s needs. Furthermore, demonstrating sensitivity to students’ cultural backgrounds and language practices promotes inclusion while improving student engagement, comfort, and participation.


4. Why must learning environments be assessment-centred?

Learning environments must be assessment-centred in order to determine whether students are progressing toward learning goals. Assessment-centred environments provide frequent opportunities for students to receive feedback, make revisions, and improve their understandings. How People Learn: Brain, Mind, Experience, and School reminds me to thoughtfully design assessments and incorporate formative assessments, observations, peer-to-peer feedback, and self-assessment. These strategies make assessments an ongoing part of the learning process, as opposed to an infrequent performative measure. As a teacher candidate, I recognize that assessments provide meaningful insights for teachers but also empower students to take ownership of their learning through metacognitive reflection and revision.

Remember, this isn't a linear process, and there is no one way. Consider the manner in which you would work through the template (the response should be 1-2 paragraphs in length).

First and foremost, I would want to begin the *overall* planning process with long-range planning and defining units that will comprehensively cover the course strands outlined in the Ontario Curriculum. To tentatively outline this, I would use the school calendar to map out the approximate duration of each unit, accounting for PA days, modified schedules, and school-wide events.. Then, for a specific unit, I would cluster or separate specific curriculum expectations into lessons. In accordance with the principles of *backward design*, I would begin a *specific lesson* planning process by identifying Ontario Curriculum expectation(s) to focus on in the lesson. As mentioned by the Understanding by Design® Framework, "In the first stage of backward design, we consider our goals, examine established content standards (national, state, province, and district), and review curriculum expectations." (McTighe and Wiggins, 2). In the Ontario Tech lesson plan template, I would begin with section 7: *Learning Goals and Success Criteria* first, being sure to write the learning goals and success criteria in student-friendly language, connect them to specific expectations, and consider using focus questions. This step also aligns with the _Plot the Path_ stage of lesson planning described in _Uncommon Sense Teaching: Practical Insights in Brain Science to Help Students Learn_.

Before completing the rest of the template, I would spend time consulting exemplars and researching effective, evidence-based teaching strategies to ensure thoughtful lesson design. I would then create both pre- and post-assessment methods aligned with the success criteria, such as observation opportunities or performance tasks, to measure student understanding. Only then would I begin to plan the learning experiences and instructional flow, following the _Minds On, Action, and Consolidation_ framework. As this process unfolds, I recognize that it is non-linear and iterative. Discussions in Foundations 1 have emphasized that effective planning involves continuous reflection and revision. For example, after outlining the _Organizing the Learning Experience_ segments, I may realize the activity addresses additional expectations, prompting a return to the _Learning Goals and Success Criteria_ section. This kind of reflective revision is something I plan to engage in throughout the entire planning process. Finally, after delivering the lesson, Section 10: _Reflective Practice_ provides an opportunity to analyze student responses, assess my instructional decisions, and revise the lesson accordingly.
